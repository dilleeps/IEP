IEP App – Smart Legal Prompts: Parent Email Templates (Product Requirements & Templates)
1. Purpose
This document defines product requirements for implementing Smart Legal Prompt–driven parent email templates within the IEP App. The goal is to empower parents to communicate effectively, document concerns, and escalate appropriately while remaining compliant with IDEA procedural safeguards.
2. Scope
These requirements cover automated, context-aware email templates tied to Smart Legal Prompts triggered by IEP data patterns, progress tracking, service delivery, and procedural compliance indicators.
3. Core Functional Requirements
•	Auto-generate parent email templates based on triggered Smart Legal Prompts
•	Pre-fill student name, school, district, dates, goals, and services including primary and secondary diagnosis or prognosis
•	Allow parent editing prior to sending
•	One-click Send + Log functionality (email archived as evidence)
•	Escalation logic if no response within configurable timelines
•	Optional CC to advocate or legal support
4. Smart Legal Prompt Email Templates
Limited Progress Detected
Requests an IEP meeting and objective progress data when meaningful progress is lacking.
Vague or Non-Measurable Goals
Requests clarification and revision of goals to meet IDEA measurability standards.
Service Reduction Risk
Requests data supporting service reductions and regression consideration.
Refusal Without Documentation
Formally requests Prior Written Notice for refused requests.
Missing or Generic Progress Reports
Requests detailed progress data and service delivery logs.
Parent Concerns Not Addressed
Ensures parent input is documented and addressed in the IEP.
Restrictive Placement (LRE Concern)
Requests justification and review of placement under LRE requirements.
Missed or Inconsistent Services
Requests service logs and compensatory services.
IEP Exit Pressure Detected
Requests reevaluation prior to exiting IEP services.
Pattern of Procedural Violations
Documents compliance concerns and corrective actions.
5. Non-Functional Requirements
•	Plain-language, legally aligned tone
•	FERPA-compliant data handling
•	Configurable by state and district rules
•	Accessible reading level (6th–8th grade)
•	Audit-ready documentation storage
6. Success Metrics
•	Increased documented parent participation
•	Reduction in undocumented refusals
•	Improved response times from schools
•	Higher parent confidence and engagement scores

IEP App – Smart Legal Prompts: User Stories & Acceptance Criteria
This document defines user stories and acceptance criteria for each major screen associated with the Smart Legal Prompts feature in the IEP App. These stories are written from a parent-first perspective and are implementation-ready for agile delivery.
1. Parent Dashboard – Alerts Overview
1.	User Story:
As a parent, I want to see clear alerts when potential IEP issues arise so that I can take timely action.
2.	Acceptance Criteria:
Alerts are visually categorized by severity (green, yellow, red).
Each alert clearly states the issue in plain language.
Parent can click an alert to view detailed context.
Multiple alerts can exist simultaneously.
Alert history is retained and timestamped.
2. Smart Legal Prompt Detail Screen
3.	User Story:
As a parent, I want to understand why an alert matters legally and what questions to ask.
4.	Acceptance Criteria:
Screen displays issue summary, affected goals/services, and dates.
Legal context is shown in parent-friendly language.
System-generated legal questions are displayed.
All content is read-only except action buttons.
Parent can navigate back to dashboard.
3. Action Selection Panel
5.	User Story:
As a parent, I want guided action choices so I know what to do next.
6.	Acceptance Criteria:
Actions include Send Email, Request IEP Meeting, Download Evidence, Escalate.
Recommended action is highlighted based on risk level.
Parent can select more than one action.
Actions are logged when selected.
4. Email Review & Send Screen
7.	User Story:
As a parent, I want to review and edit emails before sending to the school.
8.	Acceptance Criteria:
Email template auto-fills student, school, and issue details.
Parent can edit subject and body.
Optional CC advocate toggle is available.
Send & Log stores email content and timestamp.
Confirmation is shown after send.
5. Escalation Guidance Modal
9.	User Story:
As a parent, I want to know when and how to escalate concerns.
10.	Acceptance Criteria:
Risk level is clearly displayed.
System recommends advocate, mediation, or monitoring.
Parent can access escalation resources.
Escalation decision is logged.
6. Evidence & Timeline Screen
11.	User Story:
As a parent, I want a clear timeline of events to support advocacy or disputes.
12.	Acceptance Criteria:
Timeline includes alerts, emails, meetings, and documents.
Entries are sorted chronologically.
Parent can download the timeline as a report.
Timeline is read-only and audit-ready.

1. Project Overview
Purpose:
Develop a secure, parent-centered mobile/web application that consolidates a child’s Individualized Education Program (IEP) information, provides time-to-time progress updates, compares progress across school years (lineage view), and supports parents with legal awareness, rights, and meeting preparation.
Objectives:
•	Empower parents to actively monitor and engage in their child’s educational progress.
•	Simplify communication between parents, teachers, and therapists.
•	Automate reminders and milestone tracking for IEP-related timelines.
•	Provide accessible, compliant, plain-language legal resources and templates.
Target Users:
•	Primary: Parent/Guardian of a child with an IEP.
•	Secondary: Teachers, Therapists, Case Managers (school staff with parent permission).
•	Optional: Advocates or Legal Support (read-only access with consent).

Step-by-Step IEP Roadmap for Parents
1. Learn the basics & your rights
•	Know the law: IDEA (Individuals with Disabilities Education Act) guarantees the right to evaluation, an IEP if eligible, FAPE (Free Appropriate Public Education), and procedural safeguards.
•	Ask for the procedural safeguards from the school — they must provide them at first referral, at the IEP meeting, and upon request.
 
2. Gather evidence & document needs (before evaluation/meeting)
•	Academic records: report cards, test scores, work samples.
•	Medical / therapy records: diagnoses, prescriptions, OT/PT/Speech notes.
•	Behavioral logs & home observations: dates, contexts, frequency.
•	Teacher emails / progress notes.
•	Assessments you’ve done at home (reading samples, checklists).
•	Put everything in one folder (paper or cloud) and keep a running IEP journal of interactions.
 
3. Request evaluation (if child hasn’t been evaluated)
•	Write a dated request to your school (email + hard copy) asking for a full special education evaluation. Keep a copy.
•	Include specific concerns (what you’ve observed, when it started, how it affects learning).
•	School must respond within district/state timelines (often 15–60 school days) — ask about deadlines in writing.
Sample line: “I am requesting a full educational and related services evaluation for [child] due to concerns about [speech/social/behavior]. Please provide consent forms and the timeline for evaluation.”
 

4. Prepare for the IEP meeting
•	Read the draft IEP & evaluation reports ahead of time.
•	Make a prioritized wishlist: top 2–3 goals, top 2 accommodation needs, services you believe are necessary (e.g., speech 30 min x3/wk).
•	Bring evidence that supports each request (work samples, logs).
•	List questions for the team (assessment methods, progress measurement, who will deliver services).
•	Bring an advocate or friend if you’d like backup.
 
5. At the IEP meeting — collaborate and document
•	Team members present: special educator, general educator, school psychologist/assessor, district rep (qualified to commit resources), provider for related services, others as needed.
•	Key agenda items to address: eligibility, present levels of academic achievement and functional performance (PLAAFP), measurable annual goals, services & frequency, accommodations/modifications, placement, assessment participation, transition planning (starting at age 14–16 depending on state).
•	Ask: How will progress be measured and reported? (e.g., biweekly data points, quarterly reports).
•	Parents share the self-evaluation report that generated from current and previous (Lineage process and samples that generated) and validate them with the team members.
•	Prioritize and adjust the goals, services and if needed assessment/progress measurements.
•	Record decisions live: Ask for a copy of any agreed changes after the meeting. If you disagree, you can ask for a follow-up meeting or record dissent in the IEP.
 
6. Craft SMART IEP goals & success criteria
•	SMART goals — Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound.
o	Example: “Given 1:1 reading instruction using [program], [child] will read grade-level passages at 90 WPM with 90% accuracy on comprehension questions in 36 weeks.”
•	Define measurement: who collects data, how often, what tool. (e.g., running records every 2 weeks; teacher-collected checklist).
•	Include short-term objectives for complex goals if needed.
 
7. Specify services, accommodations, and placement
•	Related services: speech, OT, PT, counseling — specify minutes and frequency (e.g., Speech 30 min x2/wk).
•	Accommodations: extra time, small group testing, preferential seating, assistive tech.
•	Modifications vs accommodations: modifications change expectations; use sparingly and note reason.
•	Placement: general education with supports, co-teaching, resource room, separate classroom — ensure placement is the LRE (least restrictive environment).
 
8. Define progress monitoring & communication cadence
•	Agree on reporting schedule: weekly data, biweekly summaries, quarterly meetings, offline parents share progress to teacher and school
•	Specify formats: email summaries, data charts, parent-teacher notebook.
•	Set a review date (annual IEP; but you can request reviews anytime).
 
9. Transition planning (if applicable)
•	For older students include transition goals: vocational training, independent living skills, higher education planning. Start planning early (age varies by state).
 
10. Know dispute resolution & escalation
•	Ask for mediation if there’s disagreement.
•	Due process: formal complaint and hearing — last resort but available.
•	Complaints to state education agency also possible.
•	Keep careful records of all communications and meeting notes — they matter if you escalate.
 
Quick Practical Checklist (One-page you can use)
•	Request for evaluation (date sent)
•	Copies of school eval reports received (dates)
•	Draft IEP reviewed (date)
•	SMART goals drafted by parent (bring to meeting)
•	Services requested (type, frequency)
•	Accommodations requested (list)
•	Measurement & reporting cadence agreed (how/when)
•	Next IEP review date set
 
Provide Sample Parent Questions to Ask at an IEP Meeting (based on data stored/captured in the app)
•	How is my child’s current progress measured and documented?
•	What specific, measurable outcomes are we targeting this year?
•	Who will deliver each service, and with what qualifications?
•	How will school staff communicate progress and concerns to me?
•	How will transitions (between grades, settings) be handled?
•	If my child is not making progress, what is the remediation/adjustment plan?
 
Tips & Best Practices
•	Be collaborative but persistent. Schools often want to help; clear data and respectful persistence get results.
•	Use your IEP journal — date every call/email and summarize meetings.
•	Request prior written notice for any changes in services.
•	Leverage local parent centers — most states have Parent Training & Information Centers (PTIs) offering free support.
•	Consider an advocate or attorney only if you reach an impasse.
•	Ask for assistive technology evaluations early — they can open new supports.
 
Resources to Consult
•	State’s Department of Education website (IEP timelines & forms)
•	Wrightslaw (legal guidance and sample letters)
•	Parent Training & Information Centers (PTIs) - search by state
•	Local disability advocacy groups and special education attorneys (for escalations) – Either suggest or integrate legal services (eg: Legal Zoom) by partnering with a fee.

IEP App – Smart Legal Prompts Framework
Disclaimer: This is not intended to be a legal advice or service, it is giving parents what awareness to state-specific IDEA compliance rules available and provide guidance.
 
What Is a Smart Legal Prompt?
A Smart Legal Prompt is a context-aware legal nudge that:
•	Detects risk patterns in IEP data
•	Translates them into parent-friendly legal questions
•	Guides the parent on what to do next
•	Creates documentation automatically
 
Smart Legal Prompt Structure (Standard)
Each prompt has:
A. Trigger Condition (System Logic)
B. Parent-Facing Alert (Plain Language)
C. Legal Question(s) to Ask
D. Why This Matters (Legal Context)
E. Recommended Action (CTA)
F. Evidence to Collect (Auto-linked)
 
 SCENARIO → SMART LEGAL PROMPT MAPPING
 
Prompt #1: “Limited Progress Detected”
Trigger Condition
•	Same goal active > 6 months
•	Progress < expected threshold
•	Vague narrative updates detected
Parent Alert
“Your child may not be making meaningful progress on one or more IEP goals.”
Legal Questions
•	“What data shows appropriate progress toward this goal?”
•	“What is the baseline, current level, and expected growth?”
•	“Why wasn’t the IEP adjusted earlier?”
Why This Matters
IDEA requires meaningful progress, not minimal effort.
Actions
🔘 Request IEP Meeting
🔘 Ask for Prior Written Notice
🔘 Download Progress Data Summary
Evidence Auto-Linked
•	Goal history
•	Progress reports
•	Service logs
 

Prompt #2: “Vague or Non-Measurable Goals”
Trigger Condition
•	Goals missing baseline, metric, or criteria
•	No data source attached
Parent Alert
“Some IEP goals may not be measurable or legally compliant.”
Legal Questions
•	“How will this goal be objectively measured?”
•	“What data will be collected and how often?”
Why This Matters
Non-measurable goals undermine accountability.
Actions
🔘 Request Goal Rewrite
🔘 Insert Measurable Goal Template
🔘 Flag for Advocate Review
 
Prompt #3: “Service Reduction Risk”
Trigger Condition
•	Service minutes decreased
•	Supports removed
•	No regression data attached
Parent Alert
“A service was reduced or removed without documented evidence.”
Legal Questions
•	“What data supports this reduction?”
•	“How was regression risk considered?”
Why This Matters
Reductions without data may violate FAPE.
Actions
🔘 Request PWN
🔘 Ask for Trial Period
🔘 Escalate to Advocate
 
Prompt #4: “Refusal Without Documentation”
Trigger Condition
•	Parent request marked “Denied”
•	No Prior Written Notice uploaded
Parent Alert
“A request was refused without written explanation.”
Legal Questions
•	“Please provide Prior Written Notice explaining this refusal.”
Why This Matters
Verbal refusals violate procedural safeguards.
Actions
🔘 Auto-generate PWN Request Email

🔘 Advocate Referral
